For people who eat meat, intend to keep eating it, and would still rather know (and offset) the damage.

About 95% of people eat meat and aren’t planning to stop. I’m one of them. I also care about not causing pointless suffering, and this is my attempt to take the first thing seriously without pretending the second isn’t true: measure the suffering behind common foods, add up my own diet, and find out what it costs to offset. You can use it to do the same for yours.

Part three

Estimating what it costs to offset

The most effective animal charities reduce suffering remarkably cheaply. The conversion from your footprint to dollars is approximate, so this is a range.

An offset doesn't undo the harm. The animals in your diet are still farmed and killed, and nothing here changes that. It works the way a flight offset does. Someone worried about climate doesn't always stop flying, but they can fund enough emissions cuts elsewhere to balance the ledger. This balances a different ledger, measured in suffering rather than carbon, and it makes no claim to absolve.

Why it's a range, and why it's this cheap

Your footprint is in suffering-days; charities avert suffering in their own units (a hen out of a cage, a shrimp given a fast death), so the conversion can't be exact. We use conservative published cost-effectiveness figures and show a range. It's cheap because these are among the most cost-effective suffering reductions anyone has measured, not because the suffering is small. Reading the results

What holds up

Robust across every moral view: small aquatic animals cause by far the most suffering per gram of protein; large mammals the least; and changing species matters far more than changing how a species is farmed.

Genuinely uncertain: exactly how much worse shrimp is than chicken, roughly 13× to 55× depending on how you read the welfare evidence. That it's worse is settled; by how much is not.

What the three moral views mean, and which to pick

Best estimate uses Rethink Priorities' welfare ranges, their considered guess at how much each species can suffer relative to a human. If you don't have a strong view, use this. All equal counts every sentient animal the same, the most cautious stance about dismissing small minds; it pushes small animals up. By brain size discounts animals with fewer neurons hardest, the most skeptical published position. The three bracket the honest range of expert opinion; the conclusions that survive all three are the ones to trust.
